The biggest story of the week for buyers is not a single chair launch but a combination of moves that together signal where the mid-to-premium market is heading: more 4D mechanisms at lower price points, a meaningful regulatory milestone from Luraco that could change how some buyers pay for their chairs, and Human Touch completing its Ascent Series refresh with the Super Novo 3.0 now available for purchase. If you are mid-research on a chair in the $4,000 to $8,000 range, this week gave you more to evaluate than most.

## New Chairs and Launches

**Kyota Konbi M728 DualPro 4D.** The week's most-discussed launch is from [Kyota](/brands/kyota). The Konbi M728 DualPro is the brand's first chair to combine a 4D roller system with a dual-mechanism design, meaning the chair uses two separate massage carriages working in coordination rather than a single unit. According to the press release, EVP of Product David Figler described the goal as making "dual mechanism, high-end massage technology accessible to more people than ever before." Kyota has not announced a final retail price at the time of this writing, though the draft angle from the brand positions it in the $4,000 to $6,000 range. We will confirm pricing and track type once the chair ships. If the dual-mechanism execution holds up, this chair will be worth a close look from buyers who want something closer to a Bodyfriend or Furniture For Life dual-mechanism model without the corresponding price. Learn more about [4D roller technology](/learn/roller-dimensions) before comparing this to single-mechanism alternatives.

**Human Touch Super Novo 3.0.** As of March 31, 2026, [Human Touch](/brands/human-touch) has made the Super Novo 3.0 available for purchase as the newest addition to its Ascent Series. Human Touch announced this as an expansion of their premium recovery-focused lineup, building on the Novo XT that debuted at the High Point showroom earlier this year. Specific spec changes versus the prior Super Novo have not been detailed in the launch announcement beyond the Ascent Series positioning. This chair is aimed at buyers who want a premium American brand with strong design credentials. Human Touch also picked up four 2026 Platinum ADEX Awards for design excellence this week, which is consistent with the brand's emphasis on aesthetics alongside function.

**Nouhaus at High Point Market 2026.** Nouhaus, a Korean brand that has been building its North American presence since its CES 2024 ORBIT debut, showed restorative massage technology at High Point Market 2026 in April. The press release frames the product around addressing what Nouhaus calls "the sedentary health crisis." Specific new models beyond the ORBIT have not been confirmed, and we are waiting for verified specs before covering individual chairs in depth. Nouhaus is a brand worth watching in the $3,000 to $6,000 range as it builds U.S. distribution.

**Bodyfriend Falcon 2026.** Bodyfriend announced the Falcon 2026, described as a chair offering supine full-body massage. Pricing and U.S. availability have not been confirmed by the brand. Bodyfriend also separately launched a chair with an integrated body composition analyzer, according to reporting in The Korea Herald and The Investor. The body composition feature uses bioelectrical impedance or similar scanning to personalize session intensity to your current physical state, an approach that goes beyond standard shoulder-width body scanning. Whether this feature reaches U.S. buyers, and at what price, is still unclear.

**Ceragem Master V11.** Ceragem completed its Master V collection with the Master V11, rounding out a tiered lineup that now spans from the entry-level Master V5 through the flagship V11. Ceragem has been aggressively expanding its U.S. footprint, including three new direct stores in California. Specs and U.S. pricing for the V11 have not been independently confirmed.

## Technology and Innovation

**AI body scanning: what it actually does.** Several stories this week touch on AI-enabled chairs, from Gizmodo's hands-on with a $12,000 AI massage chair to Robb Report's coverage of AI pain-point detection and Straits Times reporting on stress-relief personalization. The honest buyer summary is this: AI body scanning in current chairs primarily means the chair's sensors detect your shoulder position, spine curvature, and sometimes your height and weight to adjust the roller path. More advanced implementations add heart rate monitoring or biometric feedback to dynamically shift pressure and program intensity during a session. What none of these systems currently do is replace a skilled therapist's ability to respond to real-time verbal feedback. If you are evaluating a chair that claims AI personalization, ask specifically whether the scanning happens once at session start or continuously, and whether you can override the AI's choices manually. Gizmodo's $12,000 hands-on concluded the experience was legitimately impressive but noted the premium is steep for most buyers.

**Bodyfriend's CES 2026 furniture-robot concept.** The Korea Post covered Bodyfriend's CES 2026 appearance under the headline "This is not a chair," positioning their new concept as a furniture-robot hybrid. This is more a category provocation than a near-term buying option for MCF readers. The $20,000 transformer-style robotic chair shown at CES and covered by Gadget Review and Yahoo Tech falls into the same bucket. These products matter because they signal where engineering investment is flowing, and features that debut at $20,000 often appear at $5,000 within three to four years. For now, the practical buyer should focus on proven 4D and [SL-track](/learn/track-types) systems rather than first-generation robotic concepts.

**Synca Wellness foot roller launch.** Synca Wellness announced what it is calling the only Made-in-Japan massage chair in North America to feature a foot roller. This is worth noting for buyers who specifically want plantar fascia and arch coverage as part of their massage program. The [Synca Wellness](/brands/synca-wellness) brand competes in the $3,000 to $6,000 range with a strong Japanese manufacturing story. Specs are from the brand's press release and we will verify once the chair is available for hands-on testing.

**Ceragem clinical validation.** Ceragem's thermal massage system received validation from a U.S. clinical study, as reported by Korea JoongAng Daily. Separately, the brand's recliners were recognized in a medical journal for neuroergonomics. These are meaningful third-party endorsements for a brand that has historically been stronger in Korea than in U.S. buyer awareness. Ceragem also won 12 Innovation Awards at CES 2026 and is targeting a 2028 IPO as it pivots toward AI wellness. For buyers considering Ceragem, the clinical backing for heat-based therapy is real, but U.S. distribution has historically been limited to direct stores and airport kiosks operated in partnership with Xwell.

## Brand News

**Luraco earns DME accreditation.** This is the most consequential brand news of the week for buyers who have a medical reason for their purchase. Luraco Health and Beauty, the Arlington, Texas manufacturer and the only American-made massage chair brand in the premium segment, announced on March 13, 2026 that its medical massage chairs are now officially licensed and accredited as Durable Medical Equipment (DME). DME accreditation means the chairs qualify for potential reimbursement through HSA and FSA accounts, and opens the door to insurance billing pathways in clinical and rehabilitation settings. This does not mean your insurance will automatically cover a [Luraco](/brands/luraco) chair, but it gives buyers with documented pain conditions a legitimate avenue to pursue partial reimbursement that did not exist before. It also strengthens Luraco's position in hospital and physical therapy facility procurement.

**Infinity Circadian DualFlex wins 2026 editor's pick.** [Infinity](/brands/infinity) earned International Business Times' 2026 Editor's Pick award for the Circadian DualFlex. The chair's dual-flex track system accommodates a wider range of body sizes than a fixed [SL-track](/learn/track-types), which is its primary differentiator. Third-party awards from trade publications carry moderate weight, but this one is consistent with what we have seen in hands-on evaluations of the Circadian DualFlex's fit flexibility.

**Mattress Firm and Osaki partnership.** [Osaki](/brands/osaki) continues to expand its brick-and-mortar footprint through a partnership with Mattress Firm for exclusive chairs, as reported by Furniture Today. For buyers, this means more opportunities to sit in Osaki chairs before purchasing, which matters considerably for a product where [body fit](/learn/body-fit) is a significant variable. The trade-off is that exclusive retail models sometimes carry different specs or feature sets than direct-channel versions.

**Infinity 2026 lineup at Las Vegas Furniture Market.** Infinity introduced its full 2026 product line at the Las Vegas Furniture Market, including the Presidential 2.0. The brand is also previewing a 2-in-1 power lounger that expands beyond the traditional massage chair category. Specific pricing and full spec sheets for the 2026 lineup are not yet publicly confirmed.

**Inside Edition's safety investigation** ([Inside Edition](https://news.google.com/rss/articles/CBMixwFBVV95cUxOOEE2MjlqQTZ3cEFtNUpXWWVBNnZFSjJ1RE1tZGdQcVMtSnZFYUF1R0p0eTZURE5uSXNLVWFxM3hVRjFtTFBMVENkR3BBMW85RlYtdExockRObGdqb0xqSlV0YWN1RllIaFItSEs0aDJ5X0x3ZVJPN3pCSnFmN2VDcEpTX0dVaTNsdkFDOVdyalBCYXVDc1JEV1preGMxYW8wTjVfLUFHZVhvVGZIWHpXWFoyTXAzaFoyVC1taEZUOUlpaWFXSGpn)) documented over 2,000 emergency room visits involving massage chairs in the last decade. The practical takeaway: keep hair tied back, avoid loose garments near the mechanism, and ensure the area around the chair is clear before starting a session.
