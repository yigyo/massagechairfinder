The week's most consequential story for buyers is not a single launch but a pattern: dual-mechanism chairs are arriving fast, AI body scanning is becoming table stakes in the premium tier, and Luraco just cleared a regulatory milestone that could change how some buyers pay for their chair. If you are mid-research and feeling overwhelmed by the pace of new releases, this roundup will tell you what actually matters and what you can safely ignore for now.

## New Chairs and Launches

**Kyota Konbi M728 DualPro 4D.** The loudest announcement of the week comes from [Kyota](/brands/kyota), which has officially introduced the Konbi M728 DualPro 4D. According to Kyota's EVP of Product David Figler, the goal was to make dual-mechanism, high-end massage technology accessible to more people than ever before. The "DualPro" designation refers to two independent massage mechanisms working simultaneously, one targeting the spine and the other addressing the lower body. That concept has existed in flagship Korean chairs for several years, but Kyota is positioning this model as the first time it lands at what the brand calls an "unmatched value" price point. Specific pricing has not been independently confirmed by MCF, and full specifications are pending our hands-on evaluation. If the dual-mechanism claim holds up at a realistic price, this chair is primarily for buyers who want full-body coverage without gaps between the upper and lower massage zones. We will update our [Kyota brand page](/brands/kyota) once we have verified specs.

**Human Touch Super Novo 3.0.** [Human Touch](/brands/human-touch) announced the Super Novo 3.0 as the latest addition to its Ascent Series, with availability beginning in late March 2026. The Super Novo line has a strong track record for body-scanning precision and well-tuned [SL-track](/learn/track-types) coverage. Human Touch has not yet published full spec comparisons against the outgoing Super Novo 2, so buyers should treat the 3.0 as an evolution rather than a redesign until more detail emerges. This chair is a strong consideration for buyers in the $7,000 to $9,000 range who prioritize a polished software experience and reliable customer support from a U.S.-based brand.

**Bodyfriend Falcon 2026.** Bodyfriend launched the Falcon 2026 in the Korean market, with the headline feature being what the brand describes as supine full-body massage capability, delivered in part through what Korean press has called "robotic legs." In practical terms, this means the leg assembly can reposition more dynamically than a standard calf-airbag system, allowing for compression and movement along the full lower limb rather than just the calf and foot zone. Bodyfriend chairs have historically taken 12 to 18 months to reach the U.S. market after Korean launch, so U.S. buyers should treat this as a product to track rather than act on today. Pricing and U.S. availability have not been announced.

**Fujiiryoki Cyber Relax AI.** Fujiiryoki, which launched its U.S. direct-to-consumer store at store.fujiiryoki.com in October 2025, has continued building its stateside presence with the Cyber Relax AI. According to brand materials, the chair uses AI body scanning and adaptive massage algorithms to adjust roller position, intensity, and technique in real time. Fujiiryoki is among the oldest massage chair manufacturers in the world, and their body-scanning technology has consistently been rated among the more precise in the category. No verified U.S. retail price has been confirmed for this model. Buyers who value Japanese engineering and are comfortable purchasing direct should keep this on their shortlist. Infinity also announced a 2-in-1 power lounger at a Vegas trade event, positioning it for buyers who want massage capability without giving up a functional recliner. ## Technology and Innovation

**What "dual mechanism" actually means for your body.** Both the Kyota Konbi M728 and new announcements from Furniture For Life's Positive Posture brand are using the phrase "dual mechanism" prominently. For buyers, this matters because a single [track](/learn/track-types), however well-designed, cannot simultaneously deliver deep spinal work and vigorous leg or lower-body massage. A dual-mechanism chair runs two independent roller heads, allowing the chair to address your upper back while also working your calves or hips at the same time. The result is a shorter session that covers more ground. The trade-off is higher cost and, in some designs, added mechanical complexity that requires closer attention to warranty terms. Buyers with sciatica or lower-back issues combined with tight hips or IT bands are the clearest beneficiaries.

**AI body scanning: what it does and what to ignore.** Multiple launches this week mention AI in some form, from Fujiiryoki's Cyber Relax AI to Bodyfriend's broader product roadmap. It is worth being precise about what this feature actually delivers. In current commercial chairs, AI body scanning typically means the roller mechanism performs a pre-massage pass up and down the spine, mapping shoulder width, spine length, and pressure-sensitive zones before the session begins. This improves targeting accuracy compared to manual or one-size-fits-all programs. What it does not do, in any chair currently on the market, is replace the judgment of a trained massage therapist or adapt to soft tissue changes in real time. The value is real but modest. Buyers under 5'4" or over 6'2" benefit most from accurate body scanning because these are the [body fit](/learn/body-fit) profiles most likely to be mis-served by a static roller program. For everyone in the middle of the height range, the difference between good AI scanning and a well-calibrated manual program is small.

**Bodyfriend's body composition analyzer.** Bodyfriend has added a body composition analyzer to one of its recent models, measuring metrics like body fat percentage and muscle mass via bioelectrical impedance embedded in the chair. The practical use case here is narrow: if you are using the chair as part of a structured wellness routine and want longitudinal tracking without a separate scale, this is genuinely useful. For most buyers shopping in the $3,000 to $7,000 range, it is a nice-to-have rather than a decision criterion. We will evaluate whether this feature holds up in practice when the chair reaches U.S. distribution.

**Ceragem's neuroergonomics recognition.** Ceragem chairs received recognition in a peer-reviewed medical journal for neuroergonomic design. This is a meaningful data point. Most massage chair health claims are based on user-reported outcomes or manufacturer-funded studies. Third-party journal recognition for ergonomic design properties is a higher standard of evidence, and buyers who are purchasing specifically for chronic pain management or post-surgery recovery should note it.

## Brand News

**Luraco earns DME accreditation.** [Luraco](/brands/luraco), the Texas-based manufacturer and the only massage chair brand that builds its chairs in the United States, announced on March 13, 2026 that its medical massage chairs have been officially licensed and accredited as Durable Medical Equipment. DME accreditation means that a chair may be eligible for reimbursement through HSA and FSA accounts, and in certain cases through insurance with a physician's letter of medical necessity. This is a significant development for buyers who have a documented therapeutic need and want to offset the purchase cost. Luraco chairs are already among the most clinically credible in the category. The DME status reinforces that positioning. Buyers with chronic back conditions, fibromyalgia, or neuropathy should ask their physician whether a Luraco chair qualifies for their specific insurance situation.

**Infinity and Mattress Firm partnership deepens.** Infinity has an ongoing distribution relationship with Mattress Firm, and the two companies plan additional product unveilings at a San Diego furniture fair. For buyers, this means Infinity chairs are increasingly available for in-store testing at Mattress Firm locations, which is a practical advantage given how important seat depth, shoulder width, and [room dimensions](/learn/room-fit) are to the final purchase decision. Trying a chair in person before buying is always advisable.

**Osaki Canada expands into therapy practices.** Osaki Canada unveiled a premium massage chair line aimed at professional therapy practices. This is primarily a commercial distribution story rather than a consumer product launch, but it signals that [Osaki](/brands/osaki) is actively building institutional credibility alongside its consumer business.

**Nouhaus at High Point Market 2026.** Nouhaus unveiled restorative massage technology at High Point Market in April 2026, positioning the brand's offering around the sedentary health crisis. No specific model names or verified specs have been released from this event. Nouhaus has been building its presence since its CES 2024 debut with the ORBIT, and this trade show appearance suggests the brand is investing in retail relationships. We will follow up as model details emerge.

## Worth Reading

**Wirecutter's 2026 Massage Chair Guide.** The New York Times Wirecutter has published its updated 2026 massage chair picks, narrowed to two models. [The guide is worth reading](https://www.nytimes.com/wirecutter/reviews/best-massage-chairs/) if you want to understand how a rigorous general-audience publication approaches the category. Wirecutter typically tests for ease of use and reliability over a short period rather than the long-term durability and pain-specific performance that MCF prioritizes. Their two-model approach reflects the difficulty of the category for a general audience. MCF covers a wider range because buyers in the $2,000 to $10,000+ tier have meaningfully different needs depending on height, weight, pain profile, and room constraints. Use the Wirecutter picks as a useful baseline, then use our [Chair Finder](/finder) to see whether a different model better matches your specific situation.

**CNN's massage therapist roundup.** CNN Underscored published a guide to the best massage chairs for at-home use, sourced from working massage therapists. [The article](https://www.cnn.com/cnn-underscored/health-fitness/best-massage-chairs) is useful because the therapist perspective tends to emphasize technique quality, pressure graduation, and coverage of specific muscle groups rather than spec sheets. The picks skew toward chairs that replicate manual technique most closely, which is a valuable lens distinct from the technology-forward framing most brands use in their own marketing.

**New York Magazine's expert panel.** New York Magazine published a guide sourced from massage therapists and chiropractors. [This piece](https://nymag.com) is worth skimming because chiropractor recommendations in particular tend to favor chairs that support spinal decompression and do not hyperextend the lumbar, which is a different set of criteria than general massage comfort. If you are purchasing primarily for back pain, the clinical practitioner angle is worth weighting heavily alongside consumer reviews.

**Reddit: Osaki OS-PRO Maestro LE user review.** A verified buyer on r/MassageChairs [posted a candid review of the Osaki OS-PRO Maestro LE](https://www.reddit.com/r/MassageChairs/comments/mi3qj8/review_osaki_ospro_maestro_le/) noting two specific frustrations: the chair resets intensity and 4D pressure settings to a default of 4 at the start of every session, and automated routines cap at 15 minutes without an easy extension. These are real limitations that do not show up in spec sheets or marketing materials. Buyers who plan to use long, customized sessions should factor control interface quality into their evaluation, not just roller mechanics.
