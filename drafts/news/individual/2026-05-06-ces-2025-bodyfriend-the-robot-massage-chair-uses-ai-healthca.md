Bodyfriend showcased a massage chair it calls "The Robot" at CES 2025 in Las Vegas, positioning the chair as an AI-driven healthcare device rather than a conventional luxury massage product. The announcement drew attention at the show for its emphasis on health-monitoring technology integrated directly into the massage experience.

## What Was Announced

According to Bodyfriend, "The Robot" combines AI healthcare technologies with the core massage chair function. The brand presented the chair as capable of using artificial intelligence to adapt and personalize the massage session, though the specific sensors, scanning methods, and health metrics involved have not been fully detailed in publicly available materials from the announcement.

Bodyfriend is a South Korean brand with an established presence in the premium massage chair segment, and this chair appears to represent their most ambitious push into what the industry is broadly calling "health tech" integration. Based on coverage from the CES announcement, the chair is designed to feel more like a medical or wellness device than a traditional massage product. Specific pricing, availability dates, and detailed technical specifications are pending further disclosure from the brand.

## What This Means for Buyers

The phrase "AI healthcare technology" gets used broadly in the massage chair industry, so it is worth breaking down what it can realistically mean in this context, and where genuine value lies for buyers.

At a basic level, AI body scanning in massage chairs typically means the chair uses sensors to detect your shoulder position, spine curvature, and body length before a session begins. More advanced implementations can adjust roller pressure and position in real time based on feedback during the massage. The most sophisticated versions, which Bodyfriend appears to be targeting with this chair, layer in biometric monitoring such as heart rate, stress indicators, or muscle tension readings to shape the session dynamically.

If Bodyfriend's implementation delivers on its CES framing, the buyers who would benefit most are those who want a chair that adapts to them session by session rather than running a fixed program. That includes buyers managing chronic tension or stress-related conditions who are frustrated by chairs that feel repetitive over time. It also includes households where multiple people with different body types share a single chair, since genuine AI adaptation reduces the need for manual reprogramming.

However, buyers should approach health-monitoring claims with measured expectations. A massage chair is not a medical device in the regulatory sense, and the health insights a chair can provide are generally wellness-oriented rather than clinically actionable. The value of AI integration in a chair like this is primarily in massage personalization, not in replacing a doctor or physical therapist.
