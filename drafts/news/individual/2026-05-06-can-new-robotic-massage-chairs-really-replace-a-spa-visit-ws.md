The Wall Street Journal published a consumer-focused examination of whether today's robotic massage chairs can genuinely substitute for professional spa and massage therapy visits. The piece, which referenced Daiwa among the brands evaluated, reflects a broader mainstream moment for the category as chairs have grown more sophisticated and more expensive.

## What Was Announced

This is not a product launch. The WSJ piece is a consumer review feature asking a question that many buyers in this category are already asking themselves privately: is a $3,000 to $8,000 massage chair actually worth it compared to spending that same money on regular professional massages over several years?

According to the framing of the story, newer chairs marketed as "robotic" are being positioned by brands as a credible home alternative to spa services, not just a luxury furniture item. Daiwa was among the manufacturers whose chairs appeared in the coverage, though the full details of which specific models were tested and how the evaluation was structured are not available from the summary provided.

## What This Means for Buyers

The WSJ taking this question seriously is meaningful context for anyone researching a chair right now. A few years ago, mainstream consumer journalism treated massage chairs as novelty items or office-lobby fixtures. That framing has shifted, and the shift reflects real improvements in track technology, body-scanning software, and the range of massage techniques chairs can simulate.

For buyers, the honest answer to the WSJ's central question is: it depends on what you are trying to replace.

If your spa visits are primarily about [stress relief and full-body muscle recovery](/best-massage-chairs-for-stress-relief), a well-specified L-track or SL-track chair can cover a meaningful share of that need. L-track designs extend roller coverage from the neck down through the glutes and upper hamstrings, which is the area most relevant to people who carry tension in their lower back and hips. For that specific use case, a quality chair used three to five times per week can genuinely substitute for a monthly massage appointment in terms of cumulative relief.

If your spa visits are more about therapeutic work from a trained therapist who adapts to your body in real time, a chair is a complement rather than a replacement. No current chair can identify a specific adhesion in your shoulder or adjust mid-session the way a skilled practitioner can.

The price math, however, is real. A chair in the $4,000 to $6,000 range, used regularly over five to seven years, often costs less per session than ongoing professional massage at current rates in most U.S. markets. That calculation is one reason buyers are increasingly willing to consider this category seriously, and why mainstream outlets like the WSJ are now covering it.

For buyers considering a [Daiwa massage chair](/brands/daiwa), the brand has historically positioned itself in the mid-to-upper segment of the market with an emphasis on therapeutic depth over entry-level feature counts. That positioning is relevant context if the WSJ coverage included Daiwa as one of the more capable examples tested.

Anyone using this coverage as a starting point for their own research should also consider what type of user they are. Buyers who want consistent daily use for back pain or muscle recovery have different needs than buyers who want a chair primarily for relaxation. ## How It Compares

Two chairs worth understanding as context for this conversation are the [Daiwa Pegasus 2 Smart](/chairs/daiwa-pegasus-2-smart) and the [Infinity Luminary](/chairs/infinity-luminary), both of which sit in the mid-to-upper price range where the "replace a spa visit" question becomes most credible.

The Pegasus 2 Smart uses an SL-track design and includes body-scanning technology that Daiwa says allows the chair to adjust roller positioning based on the user's spine shape. That kind of adaptability is one of the features that has improved most noticeably in recent chair generations and is central to whether a chair can approximate the personalized feel of a professional massage.

The Infinity Luminary, reviewed separately in our [full catalog](/chairs), is another chair in a similar tier that buyers comparison-shopping in this range often consider alongside Daiwa options.

Neither chair will replicate everything a skilled therapist provides. Both represent genuinely capable technology for home use, particularly for buyers whose primary goal is consistent, accessible muscle relief rather than acute therapeutic treatment.

The WSJ engaging with this question in 2026 reflects where the category actually is: past the novelty stage, not yet at the point where any chair is a complete substitute, but well within the range where a serious buyer can make a defensible case for home ownership over ongoing professional appointments.
