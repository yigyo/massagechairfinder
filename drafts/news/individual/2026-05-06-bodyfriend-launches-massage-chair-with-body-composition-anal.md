Bodyfriend, the South Korean luxury massage chair brand, has announced a new massage chair featuring an integrated body composition analyzer, according to a report in The Korea Herald published in early May 2026. The announcement signals a continued push by Bodyfriend to position its chairs at the intersection of wellness technology and therapeutic massage.

## What Was Announced

According to The Korea Herald, Bodyfriend has launched a massage chair that incorporates a body composition analyzer directly into the chair itself. Bodyfriend describes this as a way to personalize massage sessions based on the user's physical data. The specific model name and full specification list had not been published in the available source at the time of writing.

Body composition analysis typically measures metrics such as muscle mass distribution, body fat percentage, and hydration levels. According to Bodyfriend's positioning, the chair would use this data to tailor the massage program to the individual user's physical profile rather than relying solely on body scanning during the session.

Specifications are pending verification, and pricing has not been confirmed in available sources.

## What This Means for Buyers

This announcement matters because it moves massage chair personalization one step further upstream. Most high-end chairs today use real-time body scanning, typically a shoulder detection scan performed at the start of each session, to position rollers correctly along the spine. Bodyfriend's approach, if it works as described, would layer biological data on top of that positional scan. In theory, a chair that knows your muscle-to-fat ratio and hydration status could modulate massage intensity and technique in ways that a standard body scan cannot.

For buyers considering this chair, the key question is practical: does body composition data actually change how a massage chair performs in a meaningful way, or is it a sophisticated feature that adds cost without changing the day-to-day experience? At this point, that question cannot be answered without hands-on testing and a clear look at the chair's programming logic.

Bodyfriend operates at the upper end of the luxury segment. Its existing chairs, including the Phantom and the Recliner series, have historically been priced well above $5,000 and sometimes above $8,000 in the U.S. market. A chair with medical-adjacent sensing technology would likely land at the higher end of that range or above it. Buyers shopping in that price tier should weigh this announcement against established alternatives before committing.

The likely buyer for this chair is someone who already treats their home wellness setup as a health investment, not just a comfort purchase. If you are recovering from a specific injury, managing a chronic condition, or working with a coach or physical therapist who could interpret body composition data meaningfully, the feature has clear relevance. If you are primarily seeking relief from daily tension and back pain, the body composition layer may be more technology than the use case demands.
