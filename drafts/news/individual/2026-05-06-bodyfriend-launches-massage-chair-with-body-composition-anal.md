Bodyfriend, the South Korean massage chair brand known for pushing technology integration into its lineup, has announced a new massage chair that incorporates a body composition analyzer directly into the chair itself. The announcement was reported by The Korea Herald in early May 2026. No official U.S. pricing or release date has been confirmed at the time of writing.

## What Was Announced

According to The Korea Herald's coverage, Bodyfriend has launched a massage chair that pairs its massage functionality with a body composition analysis feature. According to Bodyfriend, the chair is capable of measuring body composition data, which typically includes metrics such as muscle mass, body fat percentage, and hydration levels, though the specific metrics captured by this model have not been independently verified. The chair appears positioned at the premium end of Bodyfriend's lineup, though exact pricing has not been published for the U.S. market.

Bodyfriend has a track record of integrating health-adjacent technology into its chairs. The brand has previously partnered with medical and wellness companies to add monitoring features to its products, so this announcement follows an established pattern for the brand rather than representing a complete departure from its design philosophy.

## What This Means for Buyers

This is one of the more genuinely interesting technology integrations to emerge from a major chair brand in recent years, and it deserves careful scrutiny rather than reflexive excitement.

Body composition analyzers work by sending a low-level electrical current through the body and measuring resistance, a method called bioelectrical impedance analysis. Standalone devices using this technology range from inexpensive consumer scales to clinical-grade machines. The accuracy of any BIA device depends heavily on consistent measurement conditions, hydration status, time of day, and electrode placement. Integrating this into a massage chair raises a practical question: will buyers actually use it consistently enough for the data to be meaningful?

For buyers who are already tracking fitness or managing weight-related health goals, having composition data attached to a daily wellness routine could be genuinely useful, especially if the chair's app connects that data to usage patterns or provides trend tracking over time. That kind of longitudinal data has real value. A single composition reading on any device is less meaningful than a trend line measured consistently over weeks and months.

The more important buyer question is what the chair does as a massage chair. Bodyfriend chairs in the premium tier have generally offered competitive track systems and strong build quality, but U.S. buyers have sometimes found the brand's customer support and parts availability less robust than domestic brands like [Human Touch](/reviews/human-touch) or [Infinity](/reviews/infinity). If the composition feature is the headline but the massage performance is secondary in engineering priority, that is worth knowing before purchase.

This announcement is most relevant to buyers who want their chair to serve as a broader wellness hub rather than a dedicated massage device. If you are looking primarily for the best back coverage, the deepest tissue work, or the most proven [L-track](/learn/l-track-vs-s-track) performance, the composition analyzer adds cost without directly improving the massage experience.
