A hands-on preview of the Bodyfriend Quantum surfaced from IFA 2024, where design publication Yanko Design sat in the chair and published impressions of the $15,000 robotic massage chair. The piece offers one of the few firsthand accounts of the Quantum available outside of Bodyfriend's own marketing materials, making it worth examining for serious luxury buyers.

## What Was Announced

According to the Yanko Design report, the Bodyfriend Quantum is positioned at $15,000 and was showcased at IFA 2024 as a flagship robotic massage chair. Bodyfriend is a South Korean brand with an established presence in the premium segment, and the Quantum appears to represent the top of their current lineup.

The Yanko Design hands-on described the experience as "luxurious," with the publication noting the chair's build quality and the robotic feel of its massage delivery. The report does not appear to include a detailed technical breakdown of roller type, track configuration, or airbag counts, so those specifications are pending verification from Bodyfriend directly.

## What This Means for Buyers

At $15,000, the Bodyfriend Quantum sits at the very top of the luxury massage chair market, a tier that demands serious scrutiny before purchase. A design publication describing something as "luxurious" tells you something useful about aesthetics and first impressions, but it does not answer the questions that matter most to a buyer spending this kind of money.

Here is how to think about that price point:

**Aesthetic versus therapeutic value.** IFA is a consumer electronics and design show. Publications covering it tend to evaluate products through a design and novelty lens rather than a therapeutic one. A chair can look spectacular and feel impressive for a five-minute sit-down while still underdelivering for someone using it daily to manage back pain or muscle recovery.

**Who this chair is likely for.** At $15,000, the Quantum is competing for buyers who have already decided they want a best-in-class experience and are willing to pay a significant premium for build quality, brand prestige, and advanced features. If your primary goal is therapeutic relief from a specific condition such as lower back pain, herniated discs, or sciatica, the price alone does not guarantee the Quantum is the right fit. Track type, roller intensity, and body scan accuracy matter far more than price for those use cases.

**The Korean brand premium.** Bodyfriend occupies an interesting position in the market. The brand is well known in South Korea, where massage chair culture is deeply embedded, but its U.S. retail presence and long-term support infrastructure are less established than competitors like [Inada](/reviews/inada) or brands with wider domestic dealer networks. That is a real consideration for a $15,000 purchase with a long expected service life.

For buyers in the luxury segment, the Quantum deserves consideration alongside established alternatives, but the Yanko Design preview alone is not enough to make a recommendation.

## What We Do Not Know Yet

Several important questions remain unanswered based on the available information.

**Track type and roller path.** Whether the Quantum uses an L-track, S-track, or a proprietary extended track configuration has not been confirmed in the available reporting. This is one of the most important specs for buyers with lower back or glute tension.

**Verified specifications.** Roller dimensions, airbag count, body scan method, and weight and height range are all pending verification. The Yanko Design piece did not include a specification breakdown, and Bodyfriend has not published a confirmed U.S. spec sheet through sources we have reviewed.

**U.S. availability and warranty terms.** It is not confirmed whether the Quantum is available for purchase in the United States, what the warranty terms are, or what in-home service options exist for U.S. buyers. These are critical factors at this price point.

**Long-term reliability data.** As a relatively newer model from a brand without broad U.S. market history, there is no meaningful owner reliability data available yet.

## How It Compares

Two chairs are worth mentioning in the context of a $15,000 luxury purchase.

The [Inada Dreamwave](/reviews/inada-dreamwave) has long been one of the most respected chairs in the premium segment, with a strong reputation for build quality and therapeutic effectiveness. Inada's U.S. service infrastructure is more established than Bodyfriend's, which matters when something needs repair five years from now.

The [Luraco i9 Max](/reviews/luraco-i9-max) is manufactured in the United States and sits in a lower price tier, but it is frequently cited for serious therapeutic performance and strong warranty support. For buyers whose primary goal is relief rather than design prestige, the i9 Max is a chair worth putting directly next to the Quantum in any comparison.

Neither of those chairs carries the same robotic aesthetic or the Bodyfriend brand cachet, but both have verifiable performance records and established support networks that a $15,000 purchase deserves.

The Bodyfriend Quantum may well prove to be one of the more impressive luxury chairs in its price range once full specifications and owner feedback accumulate, but based on what is currently available, buyers should treat this as a chair to watch rather than one to buy without further research.
